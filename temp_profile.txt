2.1.1 Elephant Detection and Early Warning Using Seismic Waves
This Master of Philosophy research focused on developing a non-invasive technological approach for mitigating human–elephant conflict in Sri Lanka. The study investigated the long-range detection of elephants through seismic signals generated by elephant locomotion, which were captured, processed, and classified using a custom embedded sensing system and machine learning models.
The study was guided by two primary objectives: (1) to design and develop a passive detection system that captures the seismic signals of elephants., and (2) to develop a detection model/system which identifies seismic signatures from Sri Lankan elephants. In response to these objectives, the research introduced an instrumentation system comprising a novel geophone–sensor interface for long-range seismic signal acquisition, a novel signal processing method for event detection termed the Contextually Customized Window, and a machine learning-based framework for classifying seismic signals and identifying elephant-generated seismic events under both controlled and natural environmental conditions. The work was further extended through the application of explainable artificial intelligence techniques to interpret model decisions and identify the most influential characteristics of elephant footfall seismic signals. 
he study demonstrated strong performance outcomes as follows:
Detect an elephant locomotion at a maximum distance of 155.6 meters
The classification model performed :
99 % accuracy for controlled settings  (Tested up to 155.6 m)
73 %  accuracy for natural environment (Tested up to 140 m)
70 % accuracy for HEC-prone village areas
To the best of the authors’ knowledge at the conclusion of the MPhil study in June 2023, the reported detection range represented the maximum documented range in comparable studies involving both Asian and African elephants, while the classification performance achieved in natural environments was among the highest reported under similar conditions.
This research resulted in multiple publications in a high-impact Q1 journal ranked within the top 10% in multidisciplinary research as listed.

J. L. Wijayaraja, J. L. Wijekoon and M. Wijesundara, "Event Detection and Classification for Long Range Sensing of Elephants Using Seismic Signals," in IEEE Access, vol. 13, pp. 158366-158384, 2025, doi: 10.1109/ACCESS.2025.3607383.

Importantly, the knowledge, technical expertise, and motivation developed through this research contributed to the establishment of Farm’s Tech (Pvt) Ltd, with the aim of advancing innovative technological solutions for agricultural challenges and supporting their practical translation into real-world applications.

2.1.2 Evaluation of a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Based System for Automated Viva Assessments in STEM Education
This ongoing research investigates the use of a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based framework as an automated and scalable alternative to conventional viva voce assessments in STEM education. The study develops a structured autonomous viva assessment system that integrates vector-based retrieval, prompt engineering, few-shot calibration, and LLM-based reasoning to generate personalized viva questions, evaluate student responses, and support transparent, context-aware assessment. The proposed framework was evaluated in a real-world viva setting and demonstrated strong performance in question relevance, diversity, grading alignment, and consistency relative to manual assessment. The system was evaluated at the Sri Lanka Institute of Information Technology across multiple subject domains, involving more than 250 students. Beyond its practical applicability, the study has also resulted in the following ongoing publication:
A Hybrid LLM-Optimized Framework for Autonomous Viva Assessment Using Retrieval Augmented Generation and Prompt Engineering (IEEE Access, under review)
Research Areas: Retrieval-augmented generation, large language models, automated assessment, prompt engineering, educational AI, natural language processing, human–AI evaluation alignment, and higher education technology.
Role: Principal Investigator
Contributions: Concept development, research design, system architecture, prototype development, RAG pipeline design, prompt engineering, experimental analysis, and scholarly publication)

2.1.3 Machine Learning for Flood Forecasting and Analysis in Sri Lanka
This study investigated the application of machine learning to flood forecasting and analysis in Sri Lanka, with particular focus on water level prediction and the influence of catchment areas on flood behaviour. Conducted in collaboration with the Disaster Management Division, Ministry of Defence, Sri Lanka, the research introduced an LSTM-based prediction framework integrated with explainable artificial intelligence to improve both predictive accuracy and interpretability. A context-based preprocessing method was developed to impute missing rainfall observations according to the seasonal characteristics of the study area, thereby improving the quality of the input data used for modelling. The framework achieved low prediction error, while explainable AI analysis identified the most influential catchment contributions to flood behaviour in the target region of the Kalu Ganga River basin. The study demonstrated the value of interpretable machine learning approaches for strengthening flood forecasting and disaster preparedness in Sri Lanka. The study resulted in the following publication:
J. L. Wijayaraja, N. D. U. Gamage, P. V. N. U. Pallegangoda, N. Priyashantha, W. B. M. R. Weerasekara and P. K. W. Abeygunawardhana, "Understanding Catchment Impacts: Enhanced Water Level Prediction with LSTM and Explainable AI," 2024 6th International Conference on Advancements in Computing (ICAC), Colombo, Sri Lanka, 2024, pp. 480-485, doi: 10.1109/ICAC64487.2024.10851183.
This work was further recognized through the following award:

2.1.4 Improving the Accuracy of Automobile RPM Sensing Using Ignition Pulses from Distributor-Based Ignition Systems
This study investigated signal processing techniques to improve the accuracy of automobile RPM sensing using ignition pulses from distributor-based ignition systems. By utilizing the primary ignition pulse signal, the research proposed a less mechanically intrusive alternative to conventional sensing methods and evaluated multiple filter designs to improve measurement reliability. The findings showed that a simple RC filter could effectively mitigate noise and transients while enabling accurate and stable RPM detection, highlighting the potential of the method for embedded automotive systems and vehicle retrofitting applications. Publication arising from this study:
J. L. Wijayaraja, N. Abhayasinghe and S. Wellalage, "Towards Accurate RPM Detection Using Primary Ignition Pulses," IECON 2023- 49th Annual Conference of the IEEE Industrial Electronics Society, Singapore, Singapore, 2023, pp. 1-5, doi: 10.1109/IECON51785.2023.10312638.
This work was further recognized through the following award:
IES Student and Young Professionals Paper Assistance, 49th Annual Conference of the IEEE Industrial Electronics Society.
Research Areas: Signal processing, automotive electronics, embedded systems, electronic instrumentation, filter design
Role: Principal Investigator

2.1.7 Encrypted Radio for Defence Forces
This undergraduate research project focused on the design and development of a secure analogue radio communication platform for defence applications in Sri Lanka. Conducted in collaboration with the Centre for Defence Research and Development, Ministry of Defence, Sri Lanka, the study addressed the need for a low-cost, field-operable, and secure tactical communication system suitable for local operational requirements. The project introduced an analogue FM radio system operating in VHF and UHF bands and incorporated multiple security-oriented mechanisms, including audio encryption and frequency hopping spread spectrum, to strengthen confidentiality, integrity, and availability in tactical communication. The study further investigated transceiver selection, embedded interface design, synchronisation mechanisms, and hardware implementation, resulting in a functional prototype with strong potential for further development toward a deployable defence communication platform.
Research Areas: Embedded systems, RF communication, defence electronics, secure communication systems, frequency hopping, electronic system design
Role: Undergraduate Researcher
Contribution: Frequency hopping mechanism design, embedded system development, Firmware development, hardware prototyping, PCB design, and system integration)

2.2.2 Animal repellent system design deployment and Impact Analysis
This translational project was carried out in the capacity of Co-Founder and R&D Engineer at Farm’s Tech (Pvt) Ltd and focused on the design, development, and field deployment of animal repellent systems for agricultural settings. The systems combined acoustic and light-based deterrence mechanisms to mitigate wildlife intrusion in farmlands. The work included device engineering, field evaluation of system effectiveness, and the development of low-cost manufacturing strategies to enable scalable production. More than 400 repellent devices have since been deployed across Sri Lanka, and the technology has been recognized by relevant government organizations as a sustainable solution for animal intrusion management. A field survey representing 75 users and approximately 100 deployed devices recorded a 98% satisfaction rate, indicating strong user acceptance and practical effectiveness in real-world agricultural contexts.

2.2.4 Peacock Repellent Device: Additive Manufacturing-Friendly Mechanical Design with Flexible Structures
This ongoing project focuses on the mechanical design of an attachment for an animal repellent system developed by Farm’s Tech (Pvt) Ltd, with particular emphasis on peacock repellent applications addressing a significant agricultural challenge in Sri Lanka.
